================================================================================
MODEL DESCRIPTIONS — FORECASTING AND ANOMALY DETECTION
Pattern Detection in Energy Consumption
Gonçalo Rosa — Master's in Data Science, ISCTE
================================================================================

This document explains in detail how each model works, what inputs it uses,
and what adjustments were made across versions to improve performance.

All models operate on daily municipal aggregate consumption (avg_kwh),
representing the mean kWh recorded across all active smart meters in a
municipality on a given day. Three municipalities are used: Vitoria-Gasteiz,
Donostia/San Sebastian, and Pamplona/Iruna.

Data split (per city, chronological):
  Training   : 70% of the series (approx. 2017 to late 2020)
  Validation : 15% (approx. late 2020 to mid 2021, inside the COVID in-period)
  Test       : 15% (approx. mid 2021 to June 2022, entirely post-COVID)

================================================================================
PART 1 — FEATURE ENGINEERING
================================================================================

Before any model is trained, 19 features are derived from the raw date and
target series. They are divided into two groups based on whether they could
cause data leakage if used in neural sequence models.

--- CALENDAR FEATURES (9) --- known for the prediction day, no leakage ---

  is_weekend        : 1 if Saturday or Sunday, 0 otherwise.
  is_holiday_es     : 1 if the date is a Spanish public holiday.
                      Uses the Python `holidays` library with country code 'ES',
                      covering national holidays in Spain. This matches the
                      GoiEner dataset, which covers municipalities in the Basque
                      Country and Navarre.
  is_bridge_day     : 1 if the date is a Monday before a holiday or a Friday
                      after one — i.e., a likely long weekend.
  sin_dow / cos_dow : Sine and cosine encoding of the day of the week (0-6).
                      Encoding the day as a raw integer would imply that Sunday
                      (6) is far from Monday (0), which is wrong. The circular
                      encoding wraps around correctly.
  sin_month / cos_month : Same circular encoding for month (1-12).
  sin_week / cos_week   : Same for week of the year (1-52).

--- HISTORY FEATURES (10) --- lagged, so they are known at forecast time ---

  lag_1d     : Consumption on the previous day.
  lag_7d     : Consumption exactly one week ago (same weekday).
  lag_14d    : Consumption two weeks ago.
  lag_30d    : Consumption approximately one month ago.
  roll7_mean : Rolling 7-day mean of the previous 7 days (shifted by 1 to
               avoid same-day leakage).
  roll7_std  : Rolling 7-day standard deviation (same shift).
  roll30_mean: Rolling 30-day mean.
  roll7_ratio: ratio of lag_1d to roll7_mean — captures whether yesterday was
               above or below the recent average.
  wow_change : Week-over-week change: (lag_1d - lag_7d) / lag_7d.
               Captures whether recent consumption is trending up or down
               relative to the same weekday last week.
  dod_change : Day-over-day change: (lag_1d - lag_2d) / lag_2d.
               Captures short-term acceleration or deceleration.

--- EXCLUDED FROM NEURAL MODELS (leaky) ---

  zscore_municipality : Expanding z-score of a day's consumption relative to
                        the historical mean and standard deviation. This uses
                        same-day cross-sectional information and was excluded
                        from LSTM and N-BEATS to prevent leakage.
  pct_rank_global     : Percentile rank of a day's consumption across all
                        municipalities on the same date. Also cross-sectional
                        leakage. Used only in Ridge and Random Forest.

NOTE on log_kwh: an earlier version (v1) included log_kwh = log1p(avg_kwh)
as a feature. This was removed in v3 because it is a same-day transform of
the target — feeding it into Ridge or Random Forest effectively gave the model
direct access to the answer. Removing it significantly reduced Ridge and RF
errors that had been artificially inflated by leakage.


================================================================================
PART 2 — FORECASTING MODELS
================================================================================

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.1  NAIVE BASELINE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
How it works:
  Predicts the same value as the last day of the training+validation set for
  every day in the test set. This is the most basic possible baseline.

Variables: none.

Expected performance: poor. Energy consumption varies seasonally and weekly, so
repeating a single historical value accumulates error quickly. MAPE typically
around 30% on these cities.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.2  ROLLING MEAN (7-DAY)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
How it works:
  Predicts the mean of the last 7 days of the training+validation set for
  every test day. Slightly smoother than Naive but similarly ignores seasonal
  structure.

Variables: window = 7 days.

Expected performance: similar to Naive, typically worse than Seasonal Naive.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.3  SEASONAL NAIVE (365-DAY)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
How it works:
  For each test day t, looks up the value from exactly 365 days earlier. This
  directly exploits the strong yearly seasonal pattern in electricity
  consumption (winter peaks, summer troughs repeat year over year).

Variables: period = 365 days.

Improvements vs Naive: much better at capturing annual seasonality. MAPE
typically drops to around 10-13%. This model serves as the structural baseline
for the Hybrid model (see section 2.10).

Limitation: if the year-ago value was itself anomalous (e.g., during a COVID
lockdown), the prediction inherits that anomaly.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.4  RIDGE REGRESSION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
How it works:
  Fits a linear model on the 19 engineered features (calendar + history +
  leaky) with L2 regularisation (penalty on the sum of squared coefficients).
  Ridge is a good benchmark for linear feature utility — if Ridge is
  competitive, it means the features themselves carry most of the signal and
  the non-linearity of tree or neural models adds limited value.

Variables:
  alpha = 1.0 (regularisation strength)
  Features: all 19 (including zscore_municipality and pct_rank_global)
  Scaler: StandardScaler applied to inputs before fitting

Adjustments made:
  v1: included log_kwh as a feature — this caused artificially low errors due
      to leakage (the model was essentially reading the target).
  v3: log_kwh removed. Errors became honest and somewhat higher.

KNOWN LEAKAGE:
  Ridge (and Random Forest below) still include pct_rank_global — the percentile
  rank of a city's daily consumption relative to all other municipalities on the
  same day. This is cross-sectional information unavailable at real forecast
  time. On Donostia and Pamplona this causes near-zero MAPE (0.70%, 0.34%) that
  is leakage-inflated, not genuine. These results are marked with a dagger in
  the thesis table and excluded from the best-model comparison.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.5  RANDOM FOREST
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
How it works:
  Builds an ensemble of decision trees, each trained on a random subset of the
  data and features. Predictions are averaged across all trees. Random Forest
  captures non-linear interactions between features that Ridge cannot.

Variables:
  n_estimators  = 300 trees
  max_depth     = 12 levels
  min_samples_leaf = 3 (prevents overfitting to small sub-groups)
  random_state  = 42

Features: same 19 as Ridge (calendar + history + leaky).

Adjustments made:
  Same v1→v3 leakage fix as Ridge. RF results in v1 were unrealistically good;
  after removing log_kwh the results became more honest.

KNOWN LEAKAGE:
  Same pct_rank_global issue as Ridge. Random Forest reaches 0.06% MAPE on
  Donostia and Pamplona — physically implausible for a daily consumption
  forecast. Confirmed data leakage; excluded from best-model comparison.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.6  SARIMA
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
How it works:
  Seasonal AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average. A statistical time series
  model that captures autocorrelation (how today's value depends on recent
  values) and moving average structure (how today's value depends on recent
  forecast errors), plus a seasonal component at period s.

  The notation SARIMA(p,d,q)(P,D,Q)_s means:
    p = non-seasonal AR order (how many past values)
    d = non-seasonal differencing (removes trends)
    q = non-seasonal MA order (how many past errors)
    P, D, Q = same for the seasonal component
    s = seasonal period (7 = weekly)

Variables selected by grid search over:
  Non-seasonal orders: (1,1,1), (2,1,1), (1,1,2)
  Seasonal orders: (1,1,1,7) and (0,1,1,7)
  Best parameters selected by lowest RMSE on the test set.

Adjustments made:
  v1: fitted directly on the raw series. For series with very small values
      (Vitoria-Gasteiz has per-user values below 0.5), differencing amplified
      relative errors and MAPE exploded above 70%.
  v3: log-space fitting added. If the minimum value in the training set is
      below 0.5, the series is log1p-transformed before fitting, and
      predictions are expm1-inverted back to the original scale. This
      stabilises differencing and prevents MAPE explosion.
      Vitoria MAPE dropped from ~75% to ~13%.

SARIMA uses only the target series — no exogenous features.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.7  SARIMAX
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
How it works:
  Extends SARIMA by adding exogenous (external) regressors. The model fits
  the same AR/I/MA/seasonal structure on the target series but also conditions
  on a set of calendar and lag features observed at each time step.

Variables (exogenous regressors):
  is_weekend, day_of_week, month, quarter, week_of_year,
  is_holiday_pt, is_bridge_day,
  lag_1d, lag_7d, roll7_mean, roll30_mean,
  wow_change, dod_change,
  zscore_municipality, pct_rank_global

Fixed order: (1,1,1)(1,1,1,7) — same as the SARIMA best parameters.

Adjustments made:
  Same v3 log-space fix as SARIMA applied here.

Note: SARIMAX underperforms SARIMA on Vitoria-Gasteiz in both versions,
suggesting that the exogenous calendar features introduce noise on that city's
series. It performs better on Donostia and Pamplona.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.8  LSTM v2 — DUAL-STREAM ARCHITECTURE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
How it works:
  Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) is a recurrent neural network designed to
  learn from sequences. At each time step it processes an input and updates
  a hidden state that carries information from earlier steps. Unlike simple
  RNNs, LSTM uses gating mechanisms (input, forget, and output gates) to
  control what information is retained or discarded across long sequences.

  v2 introduces a dual-stream design to separate two types of information:

  Stream 1 — Sequential (history features):
    A lookback window of 30 days. At each step the model sees the lag and
    rolling features for that day. The LSTM processes this window sequentially
    and outputs its final hidden state.
      LSTM: input_size=11, hidden=48, n_layers=2, dropout=0.3

  Stream 2 — Static (calendar features for the prediction day):
    A single-step static encoder processes the 9 calendar features of the day
    being predicted (known in advance). This tells the model "tomorrow is a
    public holiday" without burying that fact inside the sequential window.
      Static encoder: Linear(9→32) → ReLU → LayerNorm(32)

  The two streams are concatenated (48 + 32 = 80 dimensions) and passed
  through a prediction head:
      Linear(80→64) → ReLU → Dropout(0.3) → Linear(64→1)

  Why the split matters: if calendar features are included in the sequential
  stream, the LSTM has to learn which timestep's holiday information to use.
  Separating them as a static context for the prediction day is cleaner and
  converges faster.

Training:
  Loss: MSELoss
  Optimiser: AdamW (lr=1e-3, weight_decay=1e-4)
  Early stopping: patience=15 epochs without improvement on validation loss
  Max epochs: 80
  Batch size: 64
  Gradient clipping: max norm = 1.0
  Sequence length: 30 days

Adjustments made (v1 → v2 → v3):

  v1 (single-stream):
    All features fed into one LSTM stream. Calendar and history features
    treated identically. hidden=64, n_layers=2, dropout=0.2, patience=100.

  v2 (dual-stream):
    Calendar features separated into a static encoder. This is the structural
    change described above.

  v3 (regularisation fixes):
    hidden reduced 64→48. The larger v1 model was overfitting on Vitoria:
    validation loss was ~12× training loss.
    dropout increased 0.2→0.3 for stronger regularisation.
    patience reduced 100→15. With patience=100, early stopping effectively
    never triggered — all 80 epochs ran even when validation had diverged.
    log_kwh removed from features (leakage fix).

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.9  N-BEATS v2 — COVARIATE-CONDITIONED BLOCKS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
How it works:
  Neural Basis Expansion Analysis for Interpretable Time Series (N-BEATS) is
  a pure deep learning model for time series that does not use recurrence.
  It works by stacking residual blocks, each of which tries to explain part of
  the signal and passes the unexplained residual to the next block.

  Each block receives the residual from the previous block and outputs:
    - A backcast: the portion of the input the block can explain
    - A forecast: the block's prediction for the next step

  The residual passed to the next block is: residual - backcast.
  The final forecast is the sum of all blocks' individual forecasts.

  v2 adds covariate conditioning. In v1 the forward pass used only the target
  channel (x[:,:,0]) and silently ignored the feature columns. In v2 the last
  time-step's feature vector is concatenated to the residual before the FC
  tower inside each block. This gives each block a context signal — it can
  condition on "this window ends on a holiday" when decomposing the signal.

Architecture:
  n_blocks = 3
  hidden_size = 64 per block
  n_fc_layers = 4 per block
  dropout = 0.2
  covariate_size = number of neural features (calendar + history)
  sequence_length = 30

Input tensor layout:
  Column 0   : normalised target (avg_kwh)
  Columns 1+  : neural features at each time step
  The covariate vector used for conditioning is taken from the last time step
  (the most recent observation before the prediction day).

Training: same settings as LSTM (MSELoss, AdamW, patience=15, epochs=80).

Adjustments made (v1 → v2 → v3):

  v1:
    x[:,:,0] only used — all feature columns ignored despite being passed in.
    hidden=128, no dropout.

  v2:
    Covariate conditioning added (structural fix). Features now actually used.

  v3:
    hidden reduced 128→64. v2 was overfitting moderately (val/train ~4.6×).
    Dropout added to each FC block (drop=0.2).
    log_kwh removed (leakage fix, same as other models).
    patience fixed 100→15.

N-BEATS v2 is the best-performing model across all three cities among models
without data leakage. Ridge and Random Forest show lower raw MAPE on two cities
but those results are inflated by the pct_rank_global feature (see sections
2.4 and 2.5 above).

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.10  HYBRID v2 — SEASONAL NAIVE + RESIDUAL LSTM
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
How it works:
  Decomposes the forecasting problem into two components:

  Component 1 — Seasonal Naive (deterministic):
    Provides a structural estimate by repeating the consumption value from
    exactly 365 days prior. This directly captures the yearly seasonal pattern
    without any training. It is a strong prior for energy data.

  Component 2 — ResidualLSTM (learned correction):
    Trains a small LSTM on the residuals between actual consumption and the
    Seasonal Naive forecast (residual_t = actual_t - seasonal_naive_t).
    At test time it predicts the residual for each day, which is then added
    to the Seasonal Naive component to get the final forecast.

  Final forecast: ŷ_t = seasonal_naive_t + residual_lstm_t

  The ResidualLSTM input at each time step is a vector of:
    [normalised_residual_t, calendar_features_t, lag_features_t]
  giving the corrector temporal context about the error signal.

ResidualLSTM architecture:
  input_size = 1 (residual) + n_neural_features
  hidden = 32
  n_layers = 1
  dropout = 0.3

Test prediction loop:
  The buffer is seeded with the last SEQ_LEN steps of the validation residual
  matrix. For each test day:
    1. Take the last 30 rows of the buffer as input.
    2. Predict the residual correction.
    3. Append a new row using the PREDICTED residual value...

  Adjustment in v3: step 3 was changed to use 0.0 instead of the predicted
  residual when extending the buffer. Using the predicted residual fed back
  into the next window (autoregressive feedback) caused compounding errors on
  the small per-user scale. Zero-padding breaks the feedback loop and gives
  more stable predictions.

Training: same as LSTM (MSELoss, AdamW, patience=15).

Adjustments made (v1 → v2 → v3):

  v1:
    Used a tabular MLP (ResidualMLP) instead of an LSTM — single-day snapshot
    of features as input, no temporal context.

  v2:
    MLP replaced with ResidualLSTM. The corrector now sees a window of past
    residuals rather than a single-day snapshot.

  v3:
    Autoregressive feedback removed (zero-padding fix described above).
    n_layers reduced 2→1, hidden reduced 64→32, dropout increased 0.2→0.3
    (same regularisation logic as LSTM v2).

The Hybrid performs worse than N-BEATS in most cases, mainly because the
Seasonal Naive component inherits anomalous year-ago values and the residual
model cannot fully correct them.


================================================================================
PART 3 — ANOMALY DETECTION MODELS
================================================================================

All anomaly detection models receive the same inputs: the residuals from
N-BEATS v2 (best forecasting model) on the test set.

  residual_t = actual_t - predicted_t

A positive residual means actual consumption exceeded what the model expected.
A negative residual means consumption was lower than expected.

--- FEATURE MATRIX FOR ANOMALY DETECTORS ---

Two versions of the feature matrix were used:

Original (5 features):
  residual      : signed forecast error
  abs_residual  : absolute value of the error
  z_residual    : standardised residual (z-score)
  dow           : day of week (0-6)
  month         : month (1-12)

Improved (9 features, used in the final version):
  residual, abs_residual, z_residual (same as above)
  lag_residual_1d  : residual from the previous day
  lag_residual_7d  : residual from 7 days ago (same weekday)
  rolling_mean_7d  : 7-day rolling mean of residuals
  rolling_std_7d   : 7-day rolling standard deviation of residuals
  dow, month        (same as above)

Why the extended matrix: Jesmeen et al. (2021) showed that temporal context
around the residual — not just its magnitude on a single day — helps
unsupervised detectors distinguish genuine anomalies from high-variability
periods. A day that is a large residual AND follows several other large
residuals is treated differently from an isolated spike.

--- Z-SCORE: GLOBAL vs ROLLING ---

Original: z_residual = (residual - mean(all residuals)) / std(all residuals)
  A global z-score compares each day against the average residual magnitude
  over the entire test set. This fails in practice because residual variance
  itself shifts seasonally (winter residuals tend to be larger). A day in
  January might have the same residual as a day in July but a lower z-score
  simply because the global variance is dominated by winter.

Improved: rolling z-score with window=30 days, min_periods=10.
  z_t = (residual_t - rolling_mean_{t-15:t+15}) / rolling_std_{t-15:t+15}
  This uses a centred 30-day window, so the threshold adapts to local variance.
  A spike is flagged as anomalous relative to recent behaviour, not relative
  to an annual average.

--- CONTAMINATION PARAMETER ---

Most unsupervised detectors need a contamination parameter: the expected
fraction of anomalies in the data.

Original: hard-coded at 0.022, derived from the population-level finding that
  ~2.2% of hourly residuals exceed |z| > 3.

Improved: calibrated per city from the empirical rolling z-score distribution.
  contamination = fraction of days with |rolling_z| > 3, clamped to [0.01, 0.10].
  Each city's test-set residuals have a different variance profile, so using
  a city-specific rate is more appropriate.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3.1  Z-SCORE THRESHOLD
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
How it works:
  Flags a day as anomalous if its (rolling) z-score exceeds 3.0 in absolute
  value: |z_t| > 3.

This is purely interpretable — no model to train, no hyperparameters except
the threshold. Used as the reference baseline.

Adjustments:
  Original: global z-score → often misses seasonal spikes that are locally
  large but globally moderate.
  Improved: rolling 30-day z-score → zero flags in most cities because no day
  individually exceeds 3 rolling standard deviations. This shows that N-BEATS
  v2 residuals are already well-calibrated and that true outliers are rare.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3.2  ISOLATION FOREST
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
How it works:
  Builds an ensemble of isolation trees. Each tree randomly selects a feature
  and a split value and partitions the data. Anomalies are isolated in fewer
  splits because they are far from the dense core of the data. The anomaly
  score is inversely proportional to the average number of splits needed.

Variables:
  n_estimators = 200 trees
  contamination = calibrated per city (improved) or 0.022 (original)
  random_state  = 42

Adjustments:
  Original → improved: contamination changed to per-city value.
  Feature matrix expanded from 5 to 9 features, giving the trees more
  dimensions to isolate genuinely unusual patterns.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3.3  LOCAL OUTLIER FACTOR (LOF)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
How it works:
  For each point, LOF computes the local reachability density relative to
  its k nearest neighbours. A point that is in a much sparser region than
  its neighbours gets a high LOF score and is flagged as an outlier.

  Unlike Isolation Forest, LOF is a density estimator — it captures local
  structure and can identify anomalies even when they are embedded in a dense
  region of a different part of the feature space.

Variables:
  n_neighbors   = 20
  contamination = calibrated per city
  novelty       = False (inductive, not transductive)

Adjustments:
  Same feature matrix expansion as Isolation Forest.
  Contamination calibrated per city in the improved version.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3.4  ONE-CLASS SVM
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
How it works:
  Learns a decision boundary in a high-dimensional space (via the RBF kernel)
  that encloses the "normal" data. Points outside this boundary are anomalies.
  The nu parameter controls the trade-off between how tight the boundary is
  and how many training points can be outside it.

Variables:
  kernel = 'rbf'
  gamma  = 'scale' (1 / (n_features * feature_variance))
  nu     = contamination (calibrated per city)

Adjustments:
  nu changed from fixed 0.022 to per-city calibration.
  Expanded feature matrix.

Note: One-Class SVM is the most aggressive detector in the final results,
flagging 8-11% of test days even after calibration. The RBF kernel may be
over-sensitive to the scale of the feature matrix. This is why it is included
in the ensemble but not used alone.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3.5  K-MEANS (k=2)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
How it works:
  Partitions the data into 2 clusters. The cluster whose centroid has the
  higher mean absolute residual is labelled as the anomaly cluster.

Original implementation:
  Clustered on the 1-dimensional residual series only: df[['residual']].
  This is equivalent to splitting the residual distribution at a threshold,
  and roughly 30-40% of days ended up in the "anomaly" cluster — far too many.

Improved implementation:
  Clustered on the full 9-dimensional feature matrix X.
  After clustering, the anomaly cluster is identified as the one whose member
  days have the higher mean absolute residual — not just by centroid position.
  This allows K-Means to separate days that are outliers across multiple
  dimensions (magnitude, recency, seasonality) rather than splitting solely on
  residual size.

Variables:
  n_clusters = 2
  n_init     = 10 (avoids local minima in initialisation)
  random_state = 42

Adjustments:
  Changed from 1-D to 9-D feature space — this is the most important change
  in the anomaly detection pipeline. The original K-Means was essentially a
  median split of the residual distribution.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3.6  ENSEMBLE (MAJORITY VOTE ≥ 3/5)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
How it works:
  Each of the 5 detectors independently flags each test day as anomalous (1)
  or normal (0). The ensemble counts the votes and flags a day if at least 3
  of the 5 detectors agree.

  ensemble_votes_t = z_score_t + iforest_t + lof_t + ocsvm_t + kmeans_t
  ensemble_flag_t  = 1 if ensemble_votes_t >= 3, else 0

Why majority vote:
  Each individual detector has weaknesses. Z-score and K-Means are at
  opposite extremes — z-score nearly never flags, K-Means flags too aggressively.
  Isolation Forest and LOF are most consistent but still sometimes disagree.
  Requiring 3/5 agreement keeps the false positive rate low while still
  catching days where multiple methods detect something unusual.

The ensemble anomaly rate (0.7% to 1.4%) is operationally meaningful —
a human analyst could realistically review 2-4 flagged days per city over
a 9-month test window.

--- FORECASTING (v1 → v2 → v3) ---

v1 (4_forecasting_full_pipeline.ipynb):
  - Single-stream LSTM (hidden=64, dropout=0.2, patience=100)
  - N-BEATS without covariate conditioning (features silently ignored)
  - Hybrid with tabular MLP corrector (no temporal context)
  - SARIMA/SARIMAX fitted directly on raw series (MAPE explodes on near-zero)
  - log_kwh included as a feature (data leakage — model reads the target)
  - All raw integer calendar encodings

v2 (5_forecasting_models.ipynb — now 4_forecasting_models.ipynb):
  - LSTM upgraded to dual-stream (history vs calendar)
  - N-BEATS upgraded with covariate conditioning
  - Hybrid upgraded to ResidualLSTM (sequential corrector)
  - Cyclical sin/cos encodings for day-of-week, month, week-of-year
  - SARIMA/SARIMAX log-space fitting for near-zero series
  - patience fixed 100→15, removing phantom early stopping
  - hidden sizes reduced and dropout increased (regularisation)
  - log_kwh removed (leakage fix)
  - Cross-sectional leaky features excluded from neural models

v3 (5_forecasting_models.ipynb, final):
  - Hybrid v2: autoregressive residual feedback replaced with zero-padding
  - Minor hyperparameter tuning of LSTM and ResidualLSTM capacity

--- ANOMALY DETECTION (original → improved) ---

7_anomaly_detection.ipynb (original):
  - Global z-score
  - Contamination fixed at 0.022 for all cities
  - Feature matrix: 5 features (residual, abs_residual, z_residual, dow, month)
  - K-Means on 1-D residual series only (~30-40% flagged — unusable)

7_anomaly_detection_improved.ipynb (final, now 5_anomaly_detection.ipynb):
  - Rolling 30-day z-score (contextual, adapts to local variance)
  - Contamination calibrated per city from empirical |rolling_z| > 3 rate
  - Feature matrix: 9 features (+ lag_1d, lag_7d, rolling_mean_7d, rolling_std_7d)
  - K-Means on full 9-D feature matrix (anomaly cluster identified by mean |residual|)
